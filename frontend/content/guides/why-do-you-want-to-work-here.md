"Why do you want to work here?"

Every candidate knows this question is coming, and yet most answers sound like they were copied from the company's About page. "I admire your mission." "I love your culture." "You're an industry leader."

These answers are forgettable because they could apply to dozens of companies. The interviewer has heard them a hundred times. What they actually want is evidence that you've thought specifically about why *this* company, *this* team, *this* role — and that your reasons go beyond "you called me back."

## What They're Really Asking

This question tests three things:

- **Have you done your homework?** Do you actually know what they do, or are you shotgun-applying everywhere?
- **Is this a mutual fit?** Are your motivations aligned with what the company offers?
- **Will you be engaged?** People who have genuine reasons to be somewhere tend to work harder and stay longer.

## The Framework: Specific + Personal + Forward

Great answers have three layers:

### 1. Something Specific About the Company

Name a product, a recent initiative, a technical decision, a market position — something that proves you've done research beyond reading the first paragraph of their homepage.

### 2. Why It's Personal to You

Connect that specific thing to your experience, interests, or values. This is what makes your answer impossible to copy.

### 3. What You'd Contribute

Briefly tie it forward — what excites you about contributing to what they're building.

## Example Answers

### Example 1: Software Engineer at a Fintech Company

"I've been following your open-source work on your payments SDK for a while — I actually used it in a side project last year. The developer experience is noticeably better than the alternatives, and I know that doesn't happen by accident. It tells me your engineering team cares about craft, not just shipping features.

I spent the last three years building payment infrastructure at my current company, so I know how hard these problems are at scale. I'd love to bring that experience here and work on the kinds of distributed systems challenges that your transaction volume creates. The combination of technical depth and real-world financial impact is exactly what I'm looking for."

**Why it works:** Names a specific product. Shows genuine familiarity. Connects personal experience to the company's work.

### Example 2: Product Manager at a Health Tech Startup

"My mom is a nurse, and I grew up hearing about how broken hospital software is — clinicians spend more time fighting their tools than helping patients. When I read about how your platform reduced nurse documentation time by 40%, it wasn't just a stat to me. That's the kind of impact I want my work to have.

I've spent five years in product at B2B SaaS companies, and I've learned that building for complex workflows requires deep empathy for the end user. Healthcare is where I want to apply that skill. Your Series B tells me you're at the stage where product decisions really shape the company's trajectory, and I want to be part of that."

**Why it works:** Deeply personal motivation. Specific knowledge of the product. Understands the company's stage.

### Example 3: Recent Graduate at a Large Tech Company

"I got into computer science because of a YouTube talk by one of your engineers on how your recommendation system works. That was three years ago, and since then I've focused my coursework and projects on machine learning and information retrieval.

I know that as a new grad, I'll have a lot to learn. But what draws me to your team specifically is the scale of the problems — the chance to work on systems that serve billions of users is something I can't get anywhere else. And from what I've heard from people on the team, there's a real culture of mentorship that would help me ramp up quickly."

**Why it works:** Shows a genuine, long-standing interest. Realistic about being junior. Mentions talking to people on the team (strong signal).

### Example 4: Marketing Manager at a DTC Brand

"I've been a customer for two years, and I remember the exact Instagram ad that got me — it was funny, it didn't feel like an ad, and I ended up buying within 10 minutes. As a marketer, I reverse-engineered your funnel after that, and I was impressed by how cohesive the experience was from ad to landing page to post-purchase email.

I've been running performance marketing at a smaller DTC brand, and I've gotten our ROAS from 2x to 5x over the past year. I'd love to bring that optimization mindset to a brand that already has strong creative instincts. The combination of your brand identity and the growth stage you're at feels like the perfect place for me to make a real impact."

**Why it works:** Customer-turned-employee is a powerful narrative. Shows marketing sophistication. Specific about what they'd bring.

## Answers That Fall Flat

### "I've heard great things about your culture"

Culture is important, but this is too vague. If you mention culture, be specific: "I talked to [name] on the team, and the way they described the code review process — collaborative, not adversarial — matched how I like to work."

### "You're an industry leader"

Says nothing. Every company thinks they're a leader. Replace with something specific they've done that impressed you.

### "I need a job and you're hiring"

Honest, but not what they want to hear. Even if it's partially true, you need a better reason than that.

### "Great benefits and work-life balance"

These matter, but leading with them signals you're more interested in perks than the work. Save this for when you're evaluating the offer.

### "I want to learn and grow"

You can learn and grow anywhere. What specifically can you learn *here* that you can't elsewhere?

## How to Research Effectively

Spend 30-60 minutes before your interview gathering ammunition:

### Company-Specific Research

- **Product:** Use their product. Read reviews. Watch demos. Understand what it actually does.
- **Recent news:** Funding rounds, product launches, partnerships, leadership changes.
- **Engineering blog / tech talks:** If they publish technical content, read it. Mentioning a specific blog post is a strong signal.
- **Glassdoor / Blind:** Understand internal culture (take with a grain of salt).

### Role-Specific Research

- **Job description:** Highlight the parts that genuinely excite you.
- **Team:** Look up the hiring manager and team members on LinkedIn. Understand what the team works on.
- **Growth stage:** Is this a new team? A scaling team? A turnaround? Your answer should reflect the context.

### Personal Connection

- **Do you use the product?** Lead with that.
- **Do you know anyone there?** Mention conversations you've had.
- **Does the mission connect to your life?** Use that story — it's memorable and authentic.

## For Different Company Types

### Startup (Pre-Series B)

Emphasize: the founding team's vision, the market opportunity, your comfort with ambiguity, the chance to have outsized impact.

"I'm drawn to early-stage companies where my work directly shapes the product. Your approach to [specific problem] is different from anyone else in the space, and I want to be part of proving that thesis."

### Growth Stage (Series B-D)

Emphasize: scaling challenges, building processes, the transition from scrappy to structured.

"You're at the stage where the problems shift from 'can we build it' to 'can we scale it,' and that's exactly the kind of challenge I find most interesting."

### Large Company (Public / Enterprise)

Emphasize: scale, resources, specific teams or products, learning from world-class peers.

"The chance to work on systems that serve millions of users, with a team that's published some of the most influential papers in this space — that's a unique opportunity."

## Practice Exercise

Before your next interview, fill in these blanks:

1. **Specific thing about the company that impresses me:** ___
2. **Why that connects to my experience or interests:** ___
3. **What I'd contribute or be excited to work on:** ___

Combine them into a 45-60 second answer and practice it out loud.

## The Bottom Line

"Why do you want to work here?" is your chance to show that you're not just looking for *a* job — you're looking for *this* job. The more specific and personal your answer, the more convincing it is.

Do the research. Find the genuine connection. Say it with conviction. Generic answers get forgotten. Specific ones get offers.
